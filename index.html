How I Approach Problems

🔍

1. Start with the real problem

I've learned that the problem people describe is rarely the actual problem. Through interviews, surveys, and just watching how people behave, I try to find what's really going on. Sometimes this completely changes the direction of a project, and that's usually a good thing.

🧠

2. Figure out the behavioral mechanics

Once I understand the problem, I map out what's actually driving the behavior. What biases are at play? What's the friction? What would make this easier? This is where my behavioral science training comes in handy. I'm not guessing; I'm applying frameworks that have been tested and validated.

🎯

3. Design something that could work

This is where insights become recommendations. Whether it's a product feature, a communication strategy, or a process change, I try to make sure every suggestion is practical and implementable. Ideas that stay on slides don't help anyone.

🧪

4. Test before committing

I'm a big believer in testing. A/B tests, small pilots, quick experiments. It's much better to find out something doesn't work with 50 users than with 50,000. Plus, testing often reveals things you never would have predicted.

📈

5. Measure what matters

I focus on behavioral outcomes, not just satisfaction surveys. Did people actually change what they do? Did adoption increase? Did the problem get solved? That's what I care about, and that's what I try to measure.

What I've learned

How I think about problems

🔬
The Research Part Finding what others miss

"Data tells you what happened. Behavioral science helps you understand why, and what to do next."

I've spent a lot of time learning how to ask better questions. In my experience, the most useful insights come from combining different methods and being genuinely curious about why people do what they do:

  • Why do doctors resist AI tools even when they're proven to be accurate?
  • Why do users abandon products they claim to love?
  • Why do some marketing campaigns that "should" work end up falling flat?

💡 At SAANS.AI, I found that senior doctors actually associated visual complexity with unreliability. The fix wasn't better features; it was fewer features, presented more clearly.

📊
Making It Matter Insights are only useful if they change something

Research that sits in a report doesn't help anyone. I try to connect every finding to something actionable.

Context Matters One size genuinely never fits all

Growing up in Rajasthan, studying in Delhi, then London, and working with teams across different markets has taught me something important: context shapes everything.

The same intervention that works brilliantly in one market might completely fail in another. Not because the features are different, but because the meaning is different.

I try to understand the systems people operate within, not just the people themselves.

🚀
Where I'm headed Building the bridge between research and real impact

I want to be the person organizations call when they need to understand the human side of complex problems. Whether that's product adoption, customer experience, organizational change, or entering new markets.

My goal is pretty simple: help turn behavioral science from something academic into something that actually gives companies an edge.

Things I'm particularly interested in:

🎯 How to make AI adoption work better (hint: it's usually not a technology problem)
🧩 Consumer insights that go deeper than what surveys can tell you
🌟 Designing for emerging markets where behavioral contexts are unique

"In a world overflowing with data, the real advantage isn't having more information. It's understanding what it actually means for human behavior."

That's what I try to do.
